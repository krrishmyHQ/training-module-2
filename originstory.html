2015 — The Spark
Two batchmates stuck in Bangalore traffic, asking the same question over and over.

Utkarsh Kawatra (ex-Helion Ventures) and Vinayak Aggarwal (ex-Goldman Sachs) had known each other since IIT Delhi. Years later, stuck in Bangalore traffic together, the same question kept coming up: why can't people work from wherever they actually want? That question is where myHQ came from.

Utkarsh and Vinayak weren't random co-founders. They'd known each other since IIT Delhi — but didn't actually start anything together back then. Utkarsh went into VC at Helion. Vinayak went to Goldman Sachs as a quant. The idea waited five years until they were both in Bangalore at the same time, frustrated by the same thing. Co-founder fit isn't a luxury — it's a precondition.

The Insight
Early 2016 — The Move
They had a friend with a café. The café had a problem. They saw an opportunity.

A friend ran a café in Delhi NCR that sat dead every morning — chairs up, tables empty, burning rent before the evening rush. Utkarsh and Vinayak looked at those empty chairs and saw a workspace. That's where myHQ actually starts.

Utkarsh walked away from a VC role at Helion Ventures. Vinayak walked away from Goldman Sachs as a Quant & Algorithmic Trading Strategist. Both jobs paid extremely well. Both came with prestige. They left to run a day-pass concept out of someone else's café. The opportunity cost of starting myHQ was very real.

The Origin
April 2016 — Founded
Free WiFi. Free coffee. Discounted menus. A place to make magic. No strings.

Come in, get WiFi, order at a discounted rate, work. No deposit, no monthly commitment. The café that used to sit empty before noon started filling up with freelancers, remote workers, and early founders — and that confirmed the core insight: idle space is wasted potential.

Two co-founders. One café. Zero employees. The first year was Utkarsh and Vinayak doing literally everything — supply, demand, customer support, ops. Most of the early signups came from Utkarsh standing at the café entrance and pitching the concept to walk-ins. There was no growth team because there was no team.

Founded
2016–17 — First Legs
No marketing budget. Just word of mouth. And it was enough.

They didn't run ads. People just told other people. One café became two, then five, then 20+ venues across Delhi NCR — Social, Philips MTV, CCD. The concept had legs; now it needed wings.

2017 — First Round
Neeraj Singh (Spinny) · Ashneer Grover (BharatPe)

The 2017 round wasn't institutional capital. It was founder-to-founder belief. Neeraj Singh was building Spinny. Ashneer Grover was about to start BharatPe. They wrote cheques because they'd run their own zero-to-one and recognised the pattern. VCs at this stage were still trying to figure out if coworking was a real category. Operators-turned-investors saw it before VCs did.

20+ Spaces 2017 Angel Round
2017–18 — Growing Up
Cafés were the start. Coworking spaces were the next step. The Day Pass is born.

Cafés could only scale so far. They moved fast: 100+ coworking spaces listed on the platform in short order. And with that came a new product — the Day Pass. Book a day, show up, work, leave. Pay only for what you use.

2018 — Second Round
Anupam Mittal

Cafés had no infrastructure for work — no power outlets, unreliable wifi, ambient noise. Coworking spaces solved that. But coworking operators still sold annual memberships. The Day Pass was the unlock: take coworking-grade space and price it like a coffee. Suddenly the addressable market wasn't just full-time freelancers — it was every professional with a laptop and one productive day to spend.

100+ Spaces Day Pass Born

2019 — VC Round
Led by India Quotient

A 40-person team needs space to operate. Theirs was scrappy: started in Utkarsh's parents' basement, then moved to a 2BHK apartment in Delhi, then to a small office. Nothing about the company's growth was funded by a flashy HQ. The team was running a workspace marketplace from rented residential rooms. The irony was deliberate — they used the product themselves before insisting clients did.

2020–21 — The Test
COVID shut everything down. Multiple buyout offers came in. They said no to every single one.

COVID didn't ease them into a tough patch — it just turned the lights off. Multiple buyout offers came in. The founders said no to every single one. Then ANAROCK came — not as a rescue, but as the right fit: India's largest CRE advisory, and they wanted myHQ to keep being myHQ. The founders stayed. The mission stayed.

Multiple buyout offers came in during COVID. Most were fire-sale offers from people who saw a discounted asset. ANAROCK was different. They're India's biggest CRE advisory — they understood real estate at the deepest level, and they wanted myHQ to keep operating as myHQ. Not absorbed. Not rebranded. The founders stayed. The team stayed. The mission stayed. That structural alignment is why this acquisition worked when most others wouldn't have.

With ANAROCK's backing, the verticals that had been forming for years were now fully live — and the team finally had the infrastructure to scale them properly.

The visible changes were the easy part — bigger team, more cities, real verticals. The invisible change was credibility on enterprise calls. What's Next — International
myHQ is taking the model global through myhqspaces.com

India is still the core market — but flexible workspace is becoming the default globally, not just here. myhqspaces.com is the international play: same model, different geographies. The bet is that what worked in India (operator-agnostic advisory + transparent inventory) translates because the underlying problem — businesses needing space without being trapped by it — is universal. Going global isn't a vanity move; it's the natural extension of a model that's already proven.
