01
The First Fork in the Road

Ishani Needs to Pick
a Type of Office.

She calls a broker. He asks: "Are you looking at flex or traditional?" Ishani has heard neither term before. Before she can answer, she needs to understand what she is choosing between. Start here.

Term 01 · Space Type
Traditional / Conventional Office
Click to expand
A commercial space rented directly from a landlord under a formal lease deed. The company takes the space bare or partly finished, fits it out at its own cost, and manages its own operations. Commitments are long, typically 3 to 9 years, and exit is expensive.
Think of it as buying a plot and building a house yourself. Full control, full cost, full risk.
Term 02 · Space Type
Flex Space / Flexible Office
Click to expand
Any workspace arrangement where a company pays for a ready office without a long-term lease. Includes managed offices and coworking. The operator fits out the space, manages operations, and charges a single monthly fee. Commitments are short, typically 1 to 24 months.
Think of it as renting a fully furnished apartment. You move in tomorrow. You leave with notice. The building problems are not yours.
Term 03 · Space Type
Managed Office
Click to expand
A private, fully fitted workspace custom-branded for one company and operated by a flex space provider like myHQ. Ishani gets her logo on the door, her layout inside, but the operator handles facility management, billing, and maintenance. This is myHQ's core product.
Private + Branded + Operated. That is the managed office in three words.
Term 04 · Space Type
Coworking Space
Click to expand
A shared floor where multiple companies or individuals work in the same open space. Hot desks, dedicated desks, and small cabins. No private floor. Best for very early stage teams under 10. Once a company crosses 20 to 25 seats, they typically want a private managed office.
Like a coffee shop built for serious work, shared by everyone.

The Building Game

Ishani Shortlists Three
Buildings. They Are Not Equal.

Ishani has decided on a managed office. Her myHQ account manager sends her three options across Bengaluru. The buildings are categorised by grade. She has never heard this term before. Here is what it means.

Term 05 · Building Classification
Building Grade (A, B, C)
Click to expand
A classification of commercial buildings based on specification, location, amenities, and tenant quality. Grade A is premium. Grade B is functional and mid-market. Grade C or independent buildings are value-first with minimal specs. Grade directly affects rent, brand signal, and client experience.
The grade tells you what you are walking clients into, not just where you are sitting.
Term 06 · Building Feature
LEED Certification
Click to expand
Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design. A globally recognised green building rating awarded by the US Green Building Council. LEED-certified buildings have lower power costs, better air quality, and provide a sustainability story for companies with ESG reporting requirements. Common in Grade A+ buildings.
MNC and enterprise clients often mandate LEED-certified buildings for their offices.

03
The Build-Out Question

The Shortlisted Space Is
a "Warm Shell." Ishani Freezes.

The property description from the broker says: "Grade B warm shell, 5,200 sqft, Rs. 68/sqft." Ishani calls her account manager. "What is a warm shell? Can I move in?" The answer changes her cost estimate completely.

Term 07 · Property Condition
Bare Shell
Click to expand
A raw, unfinished commercial space. No flooring, no false ceiling, no AC, no wiring beyond basic connections. The developer hands over the structural box. The tenant funds and executes 100% of the fit-out before a single employee can sit down.
Fit-out cost: Rs. 800 to 1,200/sqft. On 5,000 sqft, that is Rs. 40 to 60L in upfront capital.
Term 08 · Property Condition
Warm Shell
Click to expand
The landlord or developer has completed the core infrastructure: false ceiling, AC or HVAC, basic electrical conduits, and flooring screed. The tenant still needs to bring furniture, IT, partitions, and interior finishes. Liveable in weeks, not months, but not free.
Fit-out cost: Rs. 400 to 700/sqft. On 5,000 sqft, that is Rs. 20 to 35L still required.
Term 09 · Property Condition
Fitted Out / Move-In Ready
Click to expand
The space is fully furnished, IT-ready, meeting rooms built, pantry operational, branding installed. Zero capital expenditure required. Day one productivity. This is the state of every myHQ managed office. The client walks in and starts working.
Fit-out cost to client: Rs. 0. The operator has absorbed the entire CAPEX in the monthly fee structure.

04
The Efficiency Gap

Ishani Is Quoted 10,000 Sqft.
She Will Only Use 6,500.

The term sheet arrives. It says 10,000 sqft. Ishani has 50 people. She is calculating 200 sqft per person. Plenty, she thinks. Then the account manager explains how Indian commercial real estate actually measures space.

Term 10 · Area Measurement
Carpet Area
Click to expand
The actual usable floor area within the four walls of your office. The area where your team sits, walks, and works. Excludes walls, columns, and all shared infrastructure. In India, carpet area is typically 65 to 80% of the total area quoted in a lease.
If you laid a carpet inside your office walls, it would cover the carpet area. Nothing outside your walls is included.
Term 11 · Area Measurement
Super Built-Up Area (SBUA)
Click to expand
The total area on which Indian commercial rent is always quoted. It includes your carpet area plus a proportionate share of the building's common areas: lobbies, lifts, staircases, toilets, and corridors. SBUA is always larger than carpet area. This is the number in Ishani's term sheet.
SBUA = Carpet Area + your share of common areas. When someone quotes 10,000 sqft, they mean SBUA, not usable space.
Term 12 · Area Measurement
Loading Factor
Click to expand
The percentage by which carpet area is inflated to reach the SBUA. Represents your proportionate share of the building's common areas. A 30% loading factor means 30% of the area you pay for is not inside your office. Typical range in India: 20 to 35%.
Formula: Loading % = (SBUA - Carpet Area) / Carpet Area x 100
At 35% loading on 10,000 sqft: carpet area = 6,500 sqft. You are paying rent on 3,500 sqft of lobby and lift.
Term 13 · Area Measurement
Efficiency Ratio
Click to expand
The percentage of total SBUA that you actually get to use as carpet area. A higher efficiency ratio means more usable space per rupee of rent. Grade A buildings typically run 70 to 75% efficient. Grade B runs 65 to 70%. Grade C can fall below 60%.
Formula: Efficiency % = Carpet Area / SBUA
At 65% efficiency, a Rs. 90/sqft quote becomes Rs. 138/sqft effective rent per usable sqft.

05
The Cost Picture

The Term Sheet Has Six
Numbers. Ishani Knows One.

The term sheet from the operator lists: base rent, CAM, CAPEX, OPEX, Security Deposit, and an Escalation Clause. Ishani knows what rent means. She asks her account manager to decode the rest, one by one.

Term 14 · Cost Component
CAM - Common Area Maintenance
Click to expand
A monthly charge levied on top of rent to cover the upkeep of shared areas: lobbies, lifts, corridors, parking, landscaping, and security guards. CAM is quoted separately from rent in traditional leases but included in myHQ's all-in monthly fee. It is not optional and is not negotiable.
Typical range: Rs. 15 to 40/sqft/month. On 5,000 sqft at Rs. 25/sqft, that is Rs. 1.25L/month on top of rent. Always quote rent + CAM together, never rent alone.
Term 15 · Cost Component
CAPEX - Capital Expenditure
Click to expand
The one-time upfront cost to convert a space into a working office: civil work, flooring, partitions, false ceiling, furniture, IT cabling, and AV. In a traditional lease, CAPEX is entirely the tenant's burden. In a myHQ managed office, the operator has absorbed the CAPEX into the monthly fee structure. The client pays zero.
Traditional: Rs. 15 to 25L per seat. Managed office: Rs. 0 to client. This is one of the strongest arguments for flex.
Term 16 · Cost Component
OPEX - Operating Expenditure
Click to expand
Monthly recurring costs to run the office: electricity, housekeeping, internet, security personnel, pantry supplies, and minor repairs. In a traditional lease, OPEX sits entirely on the company's P&L as a separate line. In a managed office, OPEX is bundled into the operator's monthly fee. One invoice, no surprises.
Traditional: Rs. 8 to 15L/month for a 50-seat office. Managed: included. CFOs love the predictability of managed office billing.
Term 17 · Cost Component
Security Deposit
Click to expand
A refundable amount paid to the landlord or operator at the start of the agreement. Held against defaults or damage. Returned at the end of the term, subject to deductions. Capital is locked for the full tenure, creating an opportunity cost: money that could have been deployed in the business instead.
Traditional lease: 6 to 10 months of rent. myHQ agreements: typically 1 to 2 months. On 5,000 sqft at Rs. 90/sqft, the difference is Rs. 36L vs Rs. 9L locked capital.
CAM
Common Area Maintenance

Monthly charge for the lobby, lifts, corridors, and shared facilities. Not rent. Not negotiable. Clients are surprised by this in traditional leases when it is not disclosed upfront.

06
The Agreement

Ishani's Lawyer Sends
Two Documents.

One document is 48 pages. The other is 12. Both would get Ishani the same office. Her lawyer says the difference is significant. Her account manager explains why the shorter one is almost always better for a company at her stage.

Term 18 · Legal Document
Lease Agreement / Lease Deed
Click to expand
A formal legal document between a tenant and a landlord granting the right to occupy a property for a fixed term. Registered with the government, stamped, and enforced under property law. Includes lock-in periods, stamp duty obligations, and maintenance responsibilities. The longer, more complex document.
Ishani is now a tenant. Any dispute goes to civil court or arbitration. Exit before the lock-in? Pay penalty rent for the remaining locked period.
Term 19 · Legal Document
Service Agreement
Click to expand
A commercial contract between a company and a flex space operator, governed by contract law, not property law. No stamp duty in most states. Exit with 30 to 90 days notice. The operator manages any disputes with the building owner. Ishani is a client, not a tenant. This is what myHQ uses.
Ishani is a client. Any dispute with the building is handled by myHQ. She has one contact, one invoice, and a 30 to 90 day exit right.
